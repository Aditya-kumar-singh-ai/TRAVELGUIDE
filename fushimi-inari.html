Fushimi Inari Taisha

Explore the iconic shrine of Kyoto

Overview

Fushimi Inari Taisha (伏見稲荷大社) is one of Kyoto’s most famous and important Shinto shrines, dedicated to Inari, the Shinto god of rice, agriculture, and prosperity. The shrine is located at the base of Mount Inari and is known for its thousands of bright red torii gates, known as "Senbon Torii" (thousands of torii gates), that create a mesmerizing pathway up the mountain.

History

Fushimi Inari Taisha was founded in the 8th century, and its main shrine was constructed in 711 AD. It is one of the oldest and most revered Shinto shrines in Japan. The shrine complex has expanded over the centuries and today, it covers the entire Mount Inari, offering a stunning series of trails, shrines, and historical structures.

Significance

The torii gates that line the mountain trails are donated by individuals and businesses, and each gate is inscribed with the donor's name and the date of donation. This practice of donating gates is seen as an expression of gratitude or a wish for good fortune and prosperity. The Fushimi Inari Shrine is also a major pilgrimage site for Shinto practitioners and is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Visiting Fushimi Inari Taisha

Visitors can hike the entire trail up to the summit of Mount Inari, which takes about 2-3 hours. Along the way, there are several smaller shrines and resting areas. The gates are most spectacular during the early morning or late afternoon when the sunlight filters through the bright red gates, creating a mystical atmosphere. The shrine also hosts the Inari Matsuri, one of Japan’s major festivals, which takes place every February.
