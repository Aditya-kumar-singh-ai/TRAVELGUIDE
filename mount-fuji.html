Mount Fuji

The Iconic Peak of Japan

Overview

Mount Fuji (富士山, Fujisan) is the highest mountain in Japan, standing at 3,776 meters (12,389 feet). Located on Honshu Island, it is an active stratovolcano that last erupted in 1707. Mount Fuji is renowned for its symmetrical, conical shape and is considered a sacred symbol in Japanese culture.

Geography

Mount Fuji is located about 100 kilometers southwest of Tokyo and is visible from the city on clear days. The mountain sits on the border between Shizuoka and Yamanashi Prefectures. It is part of the Fuji-Hakone-Izu National Park and attracts millions of visitors annually, both for its beauty and for the opportunity to hike to its summit.

Cultural Significance

Mount Fuji has been a source of inspiration for artists, poets, and pilgrims for centuries. It holds deep spiritual significance and is considered a sacred site in Japanese Shinto and Buddhist traditions. The mountain is frequently depicted in traditional Japanese art, most famously in Hokusai’s iconic woodblock print series, "Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji."

Climbing Mount Fuji

Each year, thousands of climbers attempt to reach the summit of Mount Fuji, particularly during the official climbing season in July and August. The hike can be challenging, especially for those attempting it at night in order to see the sunrise from the top, an experience known as "Goraiko."
